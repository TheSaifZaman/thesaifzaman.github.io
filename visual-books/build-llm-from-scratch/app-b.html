B.1

Foundational Papers

The eight papers below form the intellectual lineage of modern large language models. Reading them in chronological order tells the story of how the field evolved from the Transformer architecture in 2017 to the instruction-following and parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods in use today. Each paper is publicly available on arXiv. Starting with the original Transformer paper ("Attention Is All You Need") and working forward through GPT, BERT, and their successors is the single highest-leverage reading you can do to understand everything in this book at a deeper level.

The pace of progress captured in these eight papers is remarkable. In six years (2017–2023), the field went from introducing attention as a replacement for recurrence, to demonstrating that scale alone enables few-shot learning at 175 billion parameters, to showing that efficient fine-tuning of open-weight models could match the performance of full fine-tuning at a fraction of the cost. Each of these papers is short enough to read in an afternoon and is written for a technical audience — if you can follow the code in this book, you can follow these papers.

"Attention Is All You Need" Foundation
Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar et al.  ·  2017  ·  Google Brain / Google Research
Introduced the Transformer architecture — replacing recurrent networks entirely with multi-head self-attention and positional encodings.
"BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding" Foundation
Devlin, Chang, Lee, Toutanova  ·  2018  ·  Google AI Language
Bidirectional encoder trained on masked language modelling and next-sentence prediction, establishing the pretraining + fine-tuning paradigm for NLP.
"Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training" (GPT) Foundation
Radford, Narasimhan, Salimans, Sutskever  ·  2018  ·  OpenAI
The first GPT model — demonstrated that generative pretraining on unlabelled text followed by fine-tuning on downstream tasks achieves strong performance across language benchmarks.
"Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners" (GPT-2) Foundation
Radford, Wu, Child et al.  ·  2019  ·  OpenAI
Scaled to 1.5B parameters and showed emergent zero-shot task performance — the model can perform tasks it was never explicitly trained on by conditioning on natural-language task descriptions.
"Language Models are Few-Shot Learners" (GPT-3) Foundation
Brown, Mann, Ryder et al.  ·  2020  ·  OpenAI
Scaled to 175B parameters and demonstrated strong few-shot learning — providing a few examples in the prompt is sufficient for competitive performance on many NLP tasks without any gradient updates.
"Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback" (InstructGPT) Training
Ouyang, Wu, Jiang et al.  ·  2022  ·  OpenAI
Introduced RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) to align GPT-3 with human intent, transforming a text completer into a helpful assistant — the foundation of ChatGPT.
"LLaMA: Open and Efficient Foundation Language Models" Foundation
Touvron, Lavril, Izacard et al.  ·  2023  ·  Meta AI
Open-weight LLMs from 7B to 65B parameters that match or exceed GPT-3 on benchmarks with far fewer parameters, enabling community-driven research and fine-tuning.
"LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models" Training
Hu, Shen, Wallis et al.  ·  2021  ·  Microsoft Research
Introduced low-rank decomposition matrices injected into attention layers, reducing trainable parameters by ~10,000× while matching full fine-tuning quality — making LLM fine-tuning accessible on consumer hardware.
B.2

Tools and Libraries

The following tools are the standard toolkit for LLM development in 2024–2025. PyTorch is the foundation; everything else builds on top of it. Hugging Face's Transformers library gives you access to hundreds of pretrained models with a consistent API — once you understand GPT-2 architecture from this book, you can load any Hugging Face model and fine-tune it using the same patterns. The tiktoken tokeniser is OpenAI's production BPE implementation and is what the book uses to reproduce GPT-2 tokenisation exactly.

Weights & Biases (wandb) is essential for any serious training run. It automatically logs your loss curves, learning rates, gradient norms, and system metrics (GPU utilisation, memory) in real time and stores the history so you can compare runs. After your first week of training without experiment tracking, you will never train without it again — it is simply impossible to debug training instabilities or compare hyperparameter configurations without seeing the curves. The free tier is more than sufficient for individual researchers.

Tooling
PyTorch
pytorch.org
The core deep learning framework used throughout this book. Provides tensors, autograd, nn.Module, optimizers, and GPU support.

The standard library for pretrained LLMs. Provides consistent APIs for hundreds of models including GPT-2, LLaMA, Mistral, and fine-tuning with LoRA via PEFT.

tiktoken
github.com/openai/tiktoken
OpenAI's fast BPE tokeniser used by all GPT models. Used in this book to reproduce GPT-2 tokenisation with the exact same vocabulary and merge rules.
Tooling

Experiment tracking for ML. Logs loss curves, GPU metrics, hyperparameters, and model checkpoints. Essential for comparing training runs and debugging instabilities.

Unified Python API for 100+ LLM providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Cohere, etc.). Useful for LLM-as-evaluator patterns where you call multiple providers interchangeably.
B.3

Further Learning

The resources below represent the best free learning materials available for going deeper after finishing this book. Andrej Karpathy's nanoGPT repository is probably the most valuable single resource for anyone who wants to see a clean, minimal, production-quality implementation of GPT. It is approximately 300 lines of PyTorch, fully documented, and has been used to train models up to GPT-2 scale. Reading nanoGPT after completing this book is an excellent way to consolidate your understanding and see how the patterns scale to real training runs.

The Annotated Transformer (Harvard NLP) walks through the original Transformer paper equation by equation with PyTorch code side by side. Lilian Weng's blog posts on attention mechanisms and transformers are among the most thorough and well-organised explanations in the field — her post "Attention? Attention!" alone will solidify your intuition for why multi-head attention works the way it does. Fast.ai's Practical Deep Learning course teaches neural networks bottom-up with a focus on getting things working before explaining the theory — a complementary perspective to this book's top-down approach.

nanoGPT — Andrej Karpathy Learning
github.com/karpathy/nanoGPT
A minimal, clean, ~300-line implementation of GPT in PyTorch used to train real models. The best reference for seeing how all the pieces fit together in practice.
The Annotated Transformer — Harvard NLP Learning
nlp.seas.harvard.edu/annotated-transformer
The original Transformer paper re-presented with PyTorch code interleaved with each equation — the definitive guide for understanding the architecture from first principles.
Lilian Weng's Blog — Attention and Transformer Mechanisms Learning
lilianweng.github.io
Comprehensive, mathematically rigorous blog posts on attention mechanisms, transformers, and alignment — widely considered some of the best technical writing in ML.
Practical Deep Learning for Coders — fast.ai Learning
course.fast.ai
A free course teaching deep learning from a practitioner perspective — build working models first, understand the theory second. Excellent complement to this book's architecture-first approach.

Appendix B — Reading List

Use this appendix as a reading list to deepen your understanding of the concepts in each chapter.

  • Start with "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., 2017) — it is the foundation of everything. Read it alongside The Annotated Transformer for maximum comprehension.
  • GPT → GPT-2 → GPT-3 traces the scaling story: from task-specific fine-tuning, to zero-shot capability, to few-shot learning at massive scale.
  • InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) explains how RLHF transforms a raw text completer into an assistant — the most practically impactful alignment technique deployed to date.
  • LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) is the key to democratised fine-tuning. Understanding it unlocks the ability to fine-tune 7B+ models on a single consumer GPU.
  • Tools: PyTorch + Hugging Face + tiktoken + wandb form the standard LLM development stack. Familiarity with all four is expected in production ML roles.
  • nanoGPT is the single best follow-up project after this book — implement it yourself from scratch without looking at the code, then compare your result to Karpathy's.
