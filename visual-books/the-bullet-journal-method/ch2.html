Chapter 2 The Why & Decluttering
The Why

Living with Intention

Carroll's first start-up, Paintapic, taught him a crucial lesson. Despite building a successful product, he and his partner realized they weren't passionate about it—they'd fallen in love with the process of building, not the product itself.

Intentionality is the power of the mind to direct itself toward that which it finds meaningful and take action toward that end. The opposite is operating on autopilot.

The number one regret of dying patients, recorded by palliative care nurse Bronnie Ware, was that they wished they had stayed true to themselves. Most dreams went unfulfilled due to choices made—or not made.

"Intentional living is the art of making our own choices before others' choices make us." — Richie Norton

Decluttering Your Mind

Studies suggest we have 50,000 to 70,000 thoughts per day. That is enough content to produce a book every single day. Unlike a book, our thoughts are not neatly composed. This is why we feel "busy" rather than productive.

Key distinction: "Being busy" is code for being functionally overwhelmed. Being busy is not the same thing as being productive.

Decision Fatigue

Every decision requires focus, and focus is an investment of your time and energy. The more decisions you make, the harder it becomes to make them well. This is why Barack Obama wore only gray or blue suits, and Mark Zuckerberg his gray hoodies—to limit trivial decisions.

Chapter 2 Summary

Living with Intention

The number one regret of dying patients was not staying true to themselves. Intentionality means directing your mind toward what you find meaningful, rather than operating on autopilot. You can view your BuJo as a living autobiography.

Busy vs. Productive

With 50,000-70,000 thoughts per day and countless choices, we suffer from decision fatigue. The more decisions we make, the harder they become. Leaders like Obama and Zuckerberg actively limit trivial decisions.

The Mental Inventory

Three columns: (1) Working on, (2) Should be working on, (3) Want to work on. Externalize everything from your head onto paper to get perspective and control.

The Two-Question Test

For each item: Does this matter? Is this vital? Items that fail both are distractions. Cross them off. You will be left with needs (responsibilities) and wants (goals).
