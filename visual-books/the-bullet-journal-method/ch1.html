The Origin of the Bullet Journal

Ryder Carroll created the Bullet Journal as a personal coping mechanism. Diagnosed with ADD in the 1980s, he struggled with the organizational tools available and devised his own system—a cross between a planner, diary, notebook, to-do list, and sketchbook, all in one paper notebook.

What started as private "crutches for a faulty brain" eventually became a global phenomenon. After sharing his system with a colleague in 2007, and launching BulletJournal.com, the site went from 100 to 100,000 unique visitors in days.

"Let us postpone nothing. Let us balance life's account every day. One who daily puts the finishing touches to his life is never in want of time." — Seneca, Moral Letters to Lucilius

The Promise: What BuJo Offers

The Bullet Journal method's mission is to help us become mindful about how we spend our two most valuable resources: time and energy.

The core promise: The Bullet Journal method will help you accomplish more by working on less. It helps you identify and focus on what is meaningful by stripping away what is meaningless.

Chapter 1 Summary

The Origin

Ryder Carroll, diagnosed with ADD, created the Bullet Journal as a personal system to organize his chaotic mind. It evolved from private crutches into a global methodology used by millions.

The Three Pillars

Productivity: Accomplish more by working on less, not by doing more faster.

Mindfulness: Become aware of where you are, who you are, and what you want through the act of writing by hand.

Intentionality: The power of the mind to direct itself toward what it finds meaningful and take action toward that end.

The Core Promise

BuJo helps you accomplish more by working on less. It identifies what is meaningful by stripping away what is meaningless.

System vs. Practice

The system teaches HOW (the tools and techniques). The practice teaches WHY (the philosophy and science). Together they form the complete Bullet Journal method.

Key takeaway: In the most connected time in history, we are losing touch with ourselves. The Bullet Journal provides an analog refuge to define and focus on what truly matters.
