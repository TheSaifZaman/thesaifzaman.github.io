How We Lost Barakah

~22 min read Social Analysis

1

The Macro Perspective: Societal Shifts

How did we, as an ummah, lose our connection to Barakah? The author argues that a seismic cultural shift occurred over the past two centuries. The Muslim world gradually drifted away from a life grounded in fitrah -- the natural disposition that Allah embedded in every human being. Education systems were restructured according to Western secular models, and communities began to prioritise career advancement and material success over spiritual connection and community bonds.

Timeline: How Barakah Eroded

Over two centuries, each wave of change further eroded the Barakah-centric worldview that sustained Muslim civilisation.

This was not an overnight transformation. Colonialism, globalisation, and the allure of Western modernity slowly eroded the Barakah-centric worldview that had sustained Muslim civilisation for over a thousand years. The result was a generation caught between two worlds -- neither fully grounded in Islamic values nor entirely comfortable in the secular paradigm.

At the individual level, this shift manifests as a persistent sense of emptiness despite outward success. People achieve impressive career milestones yet feel spiritually hollow. They accumulate wealth but lack contentment. The Barakah has quietly slipped away, and many do not even recognise what they have lost.

The Growing Gap

As outward career success rises across generations, inner peace and spiritual fulfilment decline -- this gap is the lost Barakah.

Layers of Fitrah Erosion

Fitrah sits at the core of every human being, but colonialism, secularism, globalisation, and materialism have stripped away its protective layers over generations.

2

When Work Becomes Religion

The author introduces us to Hanna -- a high-performing professional who wakes at 5 AM for conference calls across time zones but cannot drag herself out of bed for Fajr prayer. Hanna is not unique; she represents millions of Muslims who have unconsciously adopted "workism" as their de facto religion.

The term "workism" describes the belief that work is not just an economic necessity but the centrepiece of one's identity and purpose. Pew Research (2019) found that 95% of teens rated having a job or career they enjoy as extremely or very important to them as adults -- higher than helping others, getting married, or having children.

Hanna's Dilemma: She has essentially replaced the five daily prayers with five daily meetings. Her calendar is meticulously blocked for client calls but has no space for du'a. Her morning routine centres on email triage, not morning adhkar. She is not irreligious -- she simply allowed work to fill the spiritual vacuum.
Hanna's Day: Work Calendar vs. Prayer Schedule

The same 24 hours -- one optimised for career metrics, the other punctuated by moments of divine connection.

What Society Values vs. What Islam Values

Modern society optimises for outward metrics; Islam optimises for Barakah, contentment, and closeness to Allah.

How "Workism" Reshapes Your Identity

Workism devotes ~80% of identity to career; Islam distributes identity across worship, work, family, and community in healthy proportion.

The Five Pillars of Hustle Culture

The author identifies five toxic pillars that uphold modern hustle culture, each one undermining our connection to Barakah. Together, they form a structure that looks impressive on the outside but is spiritually bankrupt on the inside.

The Five Pillars of Hustle Culture

Five pillars hold up hustle culture -- each one corrodes a dimension of Barakah in our lives.

Materialism: Wealth becomes the primary measure of a person's worth. The Quran addresses this directly: "As for man, whenever his Lord tries him and honours him and blesses him, he says: My Lord has honoured me. But when He tries him and restricts his provision, he says: My Lord has humiliated me" (89:15-16). We have internalised the idea that more money equals more worth.

Individualism: The "self-made" myth teaches that you owe your success to no one -- not your parents, not your community, and certainly not God. This severs the gratitude loop that Barakah requires. In reality, no one is self-made; everyone is Allah-made.

Consumerism: We are trapped in an endless cycle of buying things we do not need, with money we do not have, to impress people we do not like. Each purchase promises fulfilment but delivers only a fleeting dopamine hit, followed by the desire for more.

The Consumerism Trap: An Endless Loop

The consumerism loop never leads to contentment -- only back to more wanting.

Capitalism: The author shares the famous Mexican fisherman parable: a businessman advises a happy fisherman to scale his operations, work 20-hour days for decades, and eventually retire rich -- only to do exactly what the fisherman already does every day. The system demands we sacrifice the present for a future that may never come.

Fear of Death: Terror Management Theory suggests that much of human behaviour is driven by an unconscious fear of death. We build empires, chase fame, and accumulate wealth to create an illusion of permanence. The Quran warns: "Competition in worldly increase diverts you, until you visit the graveyards" (102:1-2).

Individualism: Breaking the Gratitude Loop

The "self-made" myth breaks the gratitude loop between the servant and Allah, cutting off the flow of Barakah at its source.

The Mexican Fisherman Parable

Capitalism tells us to sacrifice decades for a future that looks identical to a present we already had.

The Merchant of Fez Story

In the 19th century, a European traveller visited Fez, Morocco, and encountered a local merchant. The traveller boasted about European progress -- railways, factories, scientific discoveries, and the relentless march of civilisation. He expected the merchant to be awed.

Instead, the Moroccan merchant listened calmly and responded with devastating simplicity:

The Merchant's Response: "You do not live longer, nor are you healthier, nor better, nor more pious, nor happier. So what is the use of all this progress?"
The Merchant's Five Criteria for True Progress

By every metric that truly matters, modern "progress" has failed -- the merchant of Fez saw this two centuries ago.

This story captures the essence of the chapter. All of modernity's achievements -- technology, industrialisation, globalisation -- have not made us live longer in meaningful ways, healthier in spirit, better in character, more pious in devotion, or happier in our hearts. We traded Barakah for "progress" and came out poorer for it.

The merchant of Fez represents a worldview where contentment, spiritual health, and closeness to Allah were the true measures of a good life -- not GDP, stock portfolios, or social media metrics.

The Merchant's Scales: Weighing True Progress

Modern civilisation piled material progress onto one side of the scale while the things that truly matter -- health, virtue, piety, happiness -- grew lighter.

The Five Pillars of Islam as Antidote

The author draws a brilliant parallel: the Five Pillars of Islam are a divinely designed antidote to the five pillars of hustle culture. Each Islamic pillar directly counters a specific toxic pillar.

Islamic Pillars Counter Hustle Culture

Each pillar of Islam is a divinely designed antidote to a pillar of hustle culture.

Shahadah counters Materialism: By declaring "La ilaha illallah," you affirm that nothing in this world deserves your ultimate devotion except Allah. This declaration shatters the idol of wealth and recentres your life around the Divine.

Salah counters Individualism: Five times a day, you stand before Allah in congregation, shoulder to shoulder with your brothers and sisters. Salah reminds you that you are not self-made -- you are part of a community, and you depend utterly on your Creator.

Zakat counters Capitalism: By mandating that 2.5% of your wealth goes to those in need, Islam disrupts the hoarding instinct that capitalism encourages. Zakat purifies your wealth and circulates Barakah through the community.

Sawm counters Consumerism: Fasting teaches you to say no to your desires. For an entire month, you practise restraint -- the exact opposite of the consumer mindset that says "buy more, eat more, want more." Ramadan is a detox from consumerism.

Hajj counters Fear of Death: Wearing the white ihram garments that resemble burial shrouds, standing on Arafat in a rehearsal of the Day of Judgement -- Hajj forces you to confront mortality directly. By facing death, you are liberated from its unconscious grip on your behaviour.

Chapter 2 Summary

  • Societal drift: The Muslim world gradually lost Barakah by shifting away from fitrah-based living and adopting secular, materialistic values through colonialism and globalisation.
  • Workism: Work has replaced religion as the source of identity and purpose for many -- exemplified by Hanna, who wakes for conference calls but not for Fajr.
  • Five Pillars of Hustle Culture: Materialism (wealth = worth), Individualism (self-made myth), Consumerism (endless buying cycle), Capitalism (sacrifice present for uncertain future), Fear of Death (chase permanence through achievements).
  • Merchant of Fez: A 19th-century Moroccan merchant challenged European "progress" -- you are not longer-lived, healthier, better, more pious, or happier, so what is the point?
  • Islamic antidotes: Shahadah counters materialism, Salah counters individualism, Zakat counters capitalism, Sawm counters consumerism, Hajj counters fear of death.
  • Quran 89:15-16: Humans equate wealth with honour and poverty with humiliation -- a sign of lost Barakah.
  • Quran 102:1-2: "Competition in worldly increase diverts you, until you visit the graveyards."

Key Terms

Fitrah
The natural disposition Allah placed in every human -- an innate orientation toward God, truth, and goodness.
Workism
The belief that work is the centrepiece of one's identity and life purpose, replacing spiritual devotion.
Hustle Culture
A societal paradigm built on materialism, individualism, consumerism, capitalism, and fear of death.
Takathur
Mutual rivalry in worldly increase -- the Quranic term (102:1) for the competitive hoarding that distracts from the hereafter.
Terror Management Theory
Psychological theory that unconscious fear of death drives much of human behaviour, including wealth accumulation and fame-seeking.
