The Power of Niyyah (Intention)

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said in the famous hadith narrated by Umar ibn Al-Khattab: "Actions are but by intentions, and every person will have only what they intended" (Bukhari and Muslim). This is considered one of the most foundational hadiths in Islam, and scholars have placed it at the very beginning of their hadith collections because of its paramount importance.

The Niyyah Transformation Engine

Niyyah is the invisible engine that transforms ordinary actions into continuous worship and reward.

Niyyah -- sincere intention -- is the invisible engine that transforms the mundane into the sacred. A person who eats to gain strength for prayer is rewarded for eating. A person who sleeps with the intention of resting their body so they can wake for Fajr is rewarded for sleeping. The same physical action -- eating, sleeping, working -- with different intentions yields completely different spiritual outcomes.

Same Action, Different Outcomes

The identical physical action of eating produces three completely different spiritual outcomes depending solely on the intention behind it.

This is the magic of Niyyah: it turns ordinary life into a continuous act of worship. When you drive to work with the intention of earning halal income to support your family for Allah's sake, even the commute becomes an act of ibadah (worship). The Barakah Culture demands that we become intentional about everything we do, starting every action with a conscious, sincere niyyah directed towards pleasing Allah.

Hadith: "Actions are but by intentions, and every person will have only what they intended. So whoever emigrated for the sake of Allah and His Messenger, then his emigration was for Allah and His Messenger. And whoever emigrated for worldly gain or to marry a woman, then his emigration was for what he emigrated for." (Bukhari & Muslim)

Seven Intentions for Every Action

The author presents a powerful method for maximising the Barakah in any action: before starting any significant task, write down multiple sincere intentions. This practice of stacking intentions multiplies the reward and Barakah of a single action many times over. The more sincere intentions you attach to an act, the more Barakah it attracts.

As an example, when writing "The Barakah Effect," the author set seven specific intentions: (1) to please Allah and seek His reward, (2) to fight Hustle Culture and its toxic productivity, (3) to reconnect the Ummah with the concept of Barakah, (4) to make Barakah accessible to every Muslim professional, (5) to encourage Muslim professionals to live purposefully, (6) to popularise the concept of Barakah globally, and (7) to serve as sadaqa jariya (continuous charity) that benefits people long after its writing.

Istikharah: Seeking Allah's Guidance

Before making any major decision, a Muslim has a powerful tool at their disposal: Salat al-Istikharah, the prayer of seeking guidance. The Prophet (peace be upon him) taught his companions to make Istikharah for all their affairs, just as he would teach them a surah from the Quran (Bukhari). This shows how central this practice is to Islamic decision-making.

Step-Through: The Istikharah Process

Start

A common misconception is that Istikharah is about seeing dreams or receiving supernatural signs. In reality, Istikharah is about surrendering the outcome to Allah. You pray two rak'ahs, then make the du'a of Istikharah, asking Allah to facilitate the matter if it is good for your religion, your livelihood, and your affairs -- and to turn it away from you if it is harmful. After making Istikharah, you proceed with the decision and trust that Allah will guide you to the best outcome.

Worldly Decision-Making vs Istikharah-Based Decision-Making

Istikharah does not replace rational decision-making -- it adds a spiritual dimension that brings peace and Barakah to the process.

Making Istikharah a habit before every significant decision -- a new job, a business venture, a marriage, a move -- is a practical way to invite Barakah into your choices. It transforms decision-making from a purely rational exercise into a spiritual act of reliance on Allah.

Asking Allah for Help (Isti'anah)

Every day, in every rak'ah of every prayer, Muslims recite: "You alone we worship, and You alone we ask for help" (Quran 1:5). This ayah from Surah Al-Fatihah encapsulates the dual relationship between the servant and Allah -- worship and seeking assistance. Isti'anah (seeking Allah's help) is not a sign of weakness; it is the highest form of spiritual intelligence.

The Two Pillars of Al-Fatihah 1:5

The servant's relationship with Allah stands on two pillars: devoted worship and humble reliance.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "Tie your camel, then put your trust in Allah" (Tirmidhi). This hadith perfectly balances effort and reliance. You do your best -- you plan, prepare, work hard -- and then you turn to Allah and ask for His help with the outcome. Du'a (supplication) is described as the weapon of the believer, and there are specific du'as for starting work, beginning a project, and seeking success.

Tie Your Camel, Then Trust: The Balance of Effort and Tawakkul

Both extremes are harmful. Islam teaches us to maximise our effort AND maximise our reliance on Allah simultaneously.

Practical du'as for work: "Bismillah, tawakkaltu 'alallah" (In the name of Allah, I place my trust in Allah) when leaving the house. "Rabbi ishrah li sadri, wa yassir li amri" (My Lord, expand my chest and ease my task for me -- Quran 20:25-26) before starting a difficult project. Making these du'as habitual invites Barakah into your daily work.

Purifying Intentions: The Danger of Riya'

The flipside of sincere intention is riya' -- showing off or performing actions for the sake of people's approval rather than Allah's pleasure. Riya' is described as the "hidden shirk" (minor polytheism) because it directs worship away from Allah towards the creation. The Prophet (peace be upon him) warned his companions that he feared riya' for them more than he feared the Dajjal (Antichrist).

One of the most chilling hadiths on this topic describes three people who will be the first to be thrown into the Hellfire on the Day of Judgement: a scholar who sought knowledge to be called learned, a generous person who gave charity to be called generous, and a warrior who fought to be called brave. In each case, the action was outwardly righteous, but the intention was corrupt -- directed towards people's praise rather than Allah's pleasure.

Sincere vs Corrupt Intention

The same outward action can lead to Paradise or Hellfire depending on its underlying intention.

The Three People with Corrupt Intentions

The hadith warns that beautiful actions driven by riya' are among the gravest spiritual dangers.

The Riya' Spectrum: From Pure to Poisoned

Intention purity exists on a spectrum. The goal is to continually renew and purify your niyyah through self-accountability.

How do you check your intentions? The author suggests asking yourself: "Would I still do this if no one ever found out?" If the answer is yes, your intention is likely sincere. Purifying intentions is an ongoing struggle -- even the salaf (pious predecessors) would constantly examine and renew their niyyah.

Chapter 6 Summary

  • Niyyah transforms the mundane into worship: The same action with different intentions yields completely different spiritual outcomes -- eating to gain strength for prayer earns reward for the eating itself.
  • Stack your intentions: Before any significant action, write down multiple sincere intentions to multiply the Barakah and reward of that single act.
  • Istikharah is surrender, not fortune-telling: Pray two rak'ahs, make the du'a, then proceed -- trusting Allah will guide you to the best outcome.
  • Isti'anah is spiritual intelligence: "You alone we worship, and You alone we ask for help" -- combining maximum effort with total reliance on Allah.
  • Tie your camel, then trust Allah: Do your best in planning and preparation, then make du'a and leave the outcome to the Divine.
  • Riya' is the hidden shirk: Actions performed for people's approval rather than Allah's pleasure are spiritually hollow and strip away Barakah.
  • The three people in Hellfire: A scholar, a generous person, and a warrior -- all punished because their beautiful actions were driven by corrupt intentions.
  • Intention check: "Would I still do this if no one ever found out?" -- a practical test for sincerity.

Key Terms

Niyyah
Intention -- the conscious purpose behind an action directed towards Allah.
Ikhlas
Sincerity -- performing actions purely for Allah's sake without any hidden motives.
Riya'
Showing off -- performing religious acts for the approval of people rather than Allah.
Istikharah
The prayer of guidance -- seeking Allah's direction before major decisions.
Isti'anah
Seeking Allah's help -- the act of turning to Allah for assistance in all affairs.
Sadaqa Jariya
Continuous charity -- an act whose reward continues to flow even after a person passes away.
