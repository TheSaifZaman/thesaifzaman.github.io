Chapter 7

Life is Not About You, it's About Serving Your Lord with Excellence

~25 min read Manifesto Element

1

Ihsan: The Highest Level of Faith

In the famous Hadith of Jibril, the angel Gabriel came to the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) in human form and asked about Islam, Iman, and Ihsan. The Prophet defined Ihsan as: "To worship Allah as if you see Him, and if you cannot see Him, then know that He sees you" (Muslim). This definition reveals Ihsan as the pinnacle of spiritual life -- a state of constant divine awareness.

Ihsan goes beyond Islam (submission through the five pillars) and beyond Iman (internal faith in the six articles of belief) to reach the highest station: excellence in everything you do, driven by the consciousness that Allah is watching. It is not a level reserved for saints and scholars -- it is an aspiration for every Muslim in every action, from prayer to professional work.

Three Levels of Faith

Ihsan is the summit of faith -- built upon the foundation of Islam and the heart of Iman.

2

Excellence in Worship

Ihsan begins in worship. How many of us rush through our salah, pecking at the ground like a crow, eager to get back to our screens? Ihsan in prayer means praying with khushu' (humble concentration) -- standing as if before a King, knowing that the King of kings is watching. It means taking time with each posture, reflecting on the words you recite, and feeling the connection.

Quran recitation with Ihsan means not just reading words but contemplating their meaning, letting them affect your heart, and allowing them to change your behaviour. Charity with Ihsan means giving with grace and gentleness, not making the recipient feel indebted, and choosing quality over quantity. Every act of worship has levels -- and Ihsan is about reaching the highest level by performing it with the awareness that Allah is watching.

Reflection: The Prophet (peace be upon him) would stand in prayer until his feet swelled, and when asked why he exerted himself so much despite being forgiven all sins, he replied: "Should I not be a grateful servant?" This is Ihsan in worship -- going far beyond the minimum because of love and gratitude.
3

Excellence in Work (Itqan)

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "Allah loves that when any of you does a job, he does it with Itqan (excellence and perfection)" (Bayhaqi). This hadith revolutionises the concept of professional work for a Muslim. It is not enough to complete a task -- you must complete it with excellence, as an offering to Allah.

Bringing Ihsan into your career means moving from "good enough" to "would this please Allah?" It means reviewing your work not just for errors, but for quality. It means treating colleagues and clients with the best manners, not because of professional obligation, but because Allah is watching. It means refusing to cut corners, even when no one would notice, because Someone always notices.

The great Islamic civilisation was built on this ethic of Itqan. The architecture of the Alhambra, the precision of Islamic geometric art, the rigour of scholars like Ibn Sina and Al-Khwarizmi -- all of these were expressions of Ihsan applied to worldly disciplines. When a Muslim approaches their work as worship, the quality of output naturally rises to an extraordinary level.

4

The Ego Trap: It's Not About You

The fundamental shift Barakah Culture demands is moving from a self-centred life to a God-centred life. When your life revolves around your ego (nafs), you are on an emotional rollercoaster -- elated by praise, crushed by criticism, anxious about status, and restless in pursuit of validation. When your life revolves around Allah (Ubudiyyah -- servitude), you find stability, purpose, and inner peace regardless of external circumstances.

Ego-Centred vs God-Centred Life

The ego-centred life is a rollercoaster of validation-seeking; the God-centred life offers profound stability.

The author poses a provocative question: how much of your daily schedule is about you -- your comfort, your image, your desires -- versus serving Allah? If we are honest, much of our busyness is ego-driven. Barakah Culture asks us to flip the script: make your life about Him, and He will take care of you.

5

Practical Ihsan: Redesigning Your Life

The author presents a powerful thought experiment: if Allah were your ultimate concern -- the centre around which your entire life revolved -- how would you rearrange your day? What would you add? What would you remove? What would you do differently?

Practical Ihsan means examining your daily routines, work habits, relationships, and time allocation through the lens of divine awareness. It means asking: "If Allah could see my browser history, my private conversations, my real motivations -- would I be proud or ashamed?" The truth is, Allah can see all of it. Ihsan is simply living as though you truly believe that.

This does not mean abandoning worldly pursuits. Rather, it means infusing them with sacred purpose. A doctor who treats patients with Ihsan sees each patient as a trust from Allah. A teacher who teaches with Ihsan sees each student as an amanah (trust). A parent who raises children with Ihsan sees parenting as their most important act of worship. When Ihsan permeates your life, Barakah follows naturally.

Thought experiment: Write down your schedule for yesterday. Next to each activity, mark whether it was primarily for your ego (E) or for Allah's sake (A). Be honest. The ratio reveals the true orientation of your life -- and the starting point for your journey toward Ihsan.

Chapter 7 Summary

  • Ihsan is the pinnacle: "To worship Allah as if you see Him" -- it transcends Islam (submission) and Iman (faith) to reach the highest station of divine awareness.
  • Excellence in worship: Pray with khushu', recite Quran with contemplation, give charity with grace -- every act has levels, and Ihsan is reaching the highest.
  • Itqan in work: "Allah loves that when any of you does a job, he does it with excellence." Move from "good enough" to "would this please Allah?"
  • Ego vs Ubudiyyah: An ego-centred life is a rollercoaster of validation; a God-centred life offers stability and purpose.
  • It's not about you: How much of your daily schedule serves your ego vs serves Allah? Flip the script.
  • Practical Ihsan: Examine your routines through the lens of divine awareness and infuse worldly pursuits with sacred purpose.
  • Islamic civilisation built on Itqan: From the Alhambra to Al-Khwarizmi -- excellence in worldly disciplines as an expression of worship.

Key Terms

Ihsan
Excellence -- worshipping Allah as if you see Him, and knowing He sees you.
Itqan
Perfection/mastery in work -- doing every task to the highest standard as worship.
Ubudiyyah
Servitude to Allah -- making your life revolve around His pleasure, not your ego.
Nafs
The ego/self -- when unchecked, it becomes the centre of a person's life instead of Allah.
Khushu'
Humble concentration and presence of heart, especially in salah.
Amanah
Trust/responsibility -- every role and relationship is a trust from Allah.
