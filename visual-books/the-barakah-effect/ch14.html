Chapter 14

Send Abundant Salawat Upon Prophet Muhammad

5 Lessons 10-Question Quiz ~25 min read Manifesto Element
14.1

The Command to Send Salawat

Allah says in the Quran: "Indeed, Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet. O you who believe, send blessings upon him and salute him with peace" (Quran 33:56). This verse is extraordinary for several reasons. It is the only act of worship where Allah Himself participates alongside the believers. Allah sends His blessings, the angels send theirs, and the believers are commanded to join in.

The significance of this cannot be overstated. Allah does not need our worship — He is free of all need. Yet here, He joins in honoring His Prophet, and then commands us to do the same. This tells us that sending Salawat is not merely a nice thing to do; it is a divine command directed at every believer: "O you who believe..."

The structure of Quran 33:56 is itself instructive. First, Allah states that He and the angels already send Salawat upon the Prophet. Then, He commands the believers to follow suit. The order is deliberate: Allah leads by example, then invites us to participate. If the Lord of all creation sends blessings upon Muhammad (peace be upon him), how much more should we?

The scholars note that this verse uses the present tense, indicating continuity. Allah continuously sends blessings upon the Prophet — it is not a one-time act. Likewise, our Salawat should be continuous, not occasional.

Quran 33:56 — Structure of the Command
Quran 33:56 — Allah leads, the angels follow, and the believers are commanded to join. A uniquely honored act of worship.
What Makes Salawat Unique Among Acts of Worship
No other act of worship has Allah Himself participating in the same action alongside the believers.
Continuous, Not Once — The Present Tense of the Verse
The Arabic present tense "yusalluna" indicates a continuous, ongoing action — not a one-time event.
14.2

The Barakah of Salawat

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "Whoever sends one blessing upon me, Allah will send ten blessings upon him" (Muslim). This is a 1:10 return ratio — for every single Salawat you send, Allah sends ten blessings back to you. In the language of Barakah, there is no investment with a higher return.

But the blessings don't stop at a 10x multiplier. Other narrations expand on what happens when you send Salawat: ten good deeds are recorded for you, ten sins are erased, and you are raised ten degrees in rank. Each Salawat is a comprehensive package of spiritual benefit that purifies, elevates, and enriches the sender.

This is one of the beautiful paradoxes of Salawat: it is a gift that benefits the giver more than the recipient. The Prophet (peace be upon him) is already in the highest station before Allah. He does not need our Salawat to increase his rank. Rather, Salawat is a gift from Allah to us — a simple, accessible act of worship that unlocks enormous Barakah in our own lives.

The scholars have cataloged numerous benefits of abundant Salawat: it is a means of having du'a answered, a source of closeness to the Prophet on the Day of Judgment, a cause for sins to be forgiven, a relief from anxiety and distress, and a key that opens doors of provision. Some scholars reported that whoever makes all their du'a into Salawat (replacing personal requests with Salawat upon the Prophet), Allah will take care of all their worldly and religious concerns.

The 1:10 Return — What One Salawat Yields
Each Salawat is a comprehensive package: blessings, good deeds, sin removal, and elevation in rank.
Broader Benefits of Abundant Salawat
The scholars catalogued these benefits: du'a acceptance, closeness to the Prophet, forgiveness, relief, provision, and all concerns handled.
The Beautiful Paradox — Who Benefits More?
The sender benefits far more than the recipient. Salawat is Allah's gift to us, disguised as an honor for the Prophet.
The 1:10 Promise: One Salawat upon the Prophet = ten blessings from Allah upon you + ten good deeds recorded + ten sins erased + ten degrees raised. No other single act of worship offers such a comprehensive, guaranteed return.

14.3

When to Send Salawat

While Salawat can and should be sent at any time, certain occasions are especially emphasized in the Sunnah and by the scholars:

Friday (Jumu'ah): The Prophet said: "Send abundant Salawat upon me on Friday, for it is witnessed by the angels" (Abu Dawud). Friday is the best day of the week, and Salawat on this day carries special weight and reward. Some scholars recommend making Friday a day of especially abundant Salawat.

After the Adhan: The Prophet said: "When you hear the mu'adhdhin, repeat what he says, then send Salawat upon me" (Muslim). This connects Salawat to the daily rhythm of prayer, ensuring it happens at least five times daily.

In Du'a: The scholars teach that du'a should be "sandwiched" between Salawat — begin with Salawat, make your request, then close with Salawat. This is because Salawat is always accepted, and a du'a placed between two accepted acts has the greatest chance of being accepted as well.

When the Prophet's name is mentioned: It is from the Sunnah to say "Sallallahu alayhi wa sallam" whenever the Prophet's name is mentioned. The Prophet said: "The miser is the one in whose presence I am mentioned and he does not send Salawat upon me" (Tirmidhi).

Key Occasions for Sending Salawat
These are the especially recommended occasions, but Salawat can be sent at any time — while walking, working, or resting.

14.4

Different Forms of Salawat

Salawat takes many beautiful forms, each with its own context and reward. The most well-known is the Ibrahimi Salawat, which is recited in every prayer (salah) during the tashahhud:

"Allahumma salli 'ala Muhammad wa 'ala aali Muhammad, kama sallayta 'ala Ibrahim wa 'ala aali Ibrahim, innaka Hameedun Majeed. Allahumma barik 'ala Muhammad wa 'ala aali Muhammad, kama barakta 'ala Ibrahim wa 'ala aali Ibrahim, innaka Hameedun Majeed."

Translation: "O Allah, send Your grace, honour, and mercy upon Muhammad and upon the family of Muhammad, as You sent Your grace, honour, and mercy upon Ibrahim and upon the family of Ibrahim. Indeed, You are Praiseworthy, Most Glorious. O Allah, send Your blessings upon Muhammad and upon the family of Muhammad, as You blessed Ibrahim and the family of Ibrahim. Indeed, You are Praiseworthy, Most Glorious."

The short form — "Sallallahu alayhi wa sallam" (May Allah send blessings and peace upon him) — is used whenever the Prophet's name is mentioned in speech or writing. Even shorter: "Allahumma salli 'ala Muhammad" suffices as a complete Salawat.

There are also longer, more elaborate forms of Salawat transmitted by various scholars, each with additional phrases of praise and honor. The beauty of Salawat is that all forms are valid and rewarded — from the simplest phrase to the most elaborate invocation. What matters most is sincerity and frequency.

Forms of Salawat — From Shortest to Longest
From the brief phrase to the full Ibrahimi Salawat, all forms are rewarded. Choose what you can sustain consistently.
The Two Halves of the Ibrahimi Salawat
The Ibrahimi Salawat has two complementary halves: asking for grace (salah) and asking for blessed increase (barakah).

14.5

Salawat as a Daily Practice

Making Salawat a daily habit — aiming for 100 or more per day — is a practice recommended by many scholars and righteous people throughout Islamic history. While there is no fixed minimum (except the obligation in prayer), making abundant Salawat a daily practice transforms your spiritual life.

The author, Mohammed Faris, shares his personal experience of how incorporating abundant daily Salawat transformed his life and brought unexpected Barakah — in his work, relationships, peace of mind, and overall sense of purpose. He found that on days when he maintained his Salawat practice, things flowed more smoothly, anxiety decreased, and opportunities appeared from unexpected directions.

Practical ways to build a Salawat habit: (1) set a daily target (start with 100, build to more), (2) use a counter (digital or physical tasbeeh), (3) attach it to existing habits (after each prayer, during commute, before sleeping), (4) make Friday your "Salawat day" with extra effort, and (5) teach your family to do the same, creating a household culture of Salawat.

The book closes with this Manifesto element as the final note — and it is fitting. Salawat upon the Prophet is the seal of Barakah, the act that ties together everything discussed in the book. It is gratitude to Allah, love for the Prophet, and investment in your own Akhirah, all in a single phrase.

Alhamdulillah — all praise is due to Allah, the Lord of all the worlds. May abundant peace and blessings be upon Prophet Muhammad, his family, his companions, and all who follow his way until the Day of Judgment.

Chapter 14 — Key Takeaways

  • Quran 33:56 is the only verse where Allah Himself participates in an act of worship alongside believers — sending blessings upon the Prophet.
  • One Salawat = ten blessings from Allah, ten good deeds, ten sins erased, ten degrees raised. No other act has a comparable guaranteed return.
  • Key occasions: Friday, after the Adhan, in du'a (open and close), when the Prophet's name is mentioned, and in times of difficulty.
  • All forms of Salawat are valid and rewarded — from the brief "Sallallahu alayhi wa sallam" to the full Ibrahimi Salawat in prayer.
  • Making 100+ Salawat a daily practice brings transformative Barakah in work, relationships, peace of mind, and provision.
  • Salawat is the fitting conclusion to the entire Barakah Manifesto — a seal that ties gratitude, love, and investment in the Akhirah together.

Key Terms

Salawat
Blessings and peace sent upon the Prophet Muhammad; a divine command (Quran 33:56) and an act of worship with immense reward.
Ibrahimi Salawat
The most well-known form of Salawat, recited in every prayer during the tashahhud, invoking blessings as Allah blessed Prophet Ibrahim.
Sallallahu Alayhi Wa Sallam
"May Allah send blessings and peace upon him" — the standard short form of Salawat used when the Prophet's name is mentioned.
Tasbeeh
Prayer beads or a counter used to track dhikr (remembrance) and Salawat; a practical aid for building daily habits.
