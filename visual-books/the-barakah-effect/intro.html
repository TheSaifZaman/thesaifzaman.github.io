The Al-Baik Secret

In 1974, Shakour AbuGhazalah had a dream: to open a high-quality, affordable fried chicken restaurant in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia. He called it "Broast" and poured his life savings into it. Unfortunately, the restaurant struggled during its first two years, and in 1976, Shakour lost a battle with cancer and died at the age of 48.

His two sons, Ihsaan and Rami, took over the business and were determined to make their father's dream a reality. They faced enormous challenges -- over 400 copycat restaurants had sprung up in Jeddah alone. Yet with sincerity and hard work, they turned this fledgling restaurant into Al-Baik, one of the most beloved fried chicken chains in Saudi Arabia.

What is the secret of Al-Baik's extraordinary success? A management consultant might point to winning recipes, branding, or location strategy. But the real secret goes deeper: when Shakour opened his restaurant, he set one simple rule -- for every fried chicken he sells, he would donate one riyal to charity. He never broke that rule, even when the business was struggling. This spiritual commitment to generosity is what attracted Barakah into the business.

What is the Barakah Effect?

A business is not a selfish endeavour. For a business to be truly successful, it needs to tap into something beyond common-sense strategy. It needs to tap into the Barakah Effect. This is the hidden, subtle element of success that boosts impact, benefit, and longevity beyond your expectations.

The Barakah Effect is the spiritual multiplier that enters your time, wealth, relationships, and work when you align your actions with the pleasure of Allah. It is the force that makes little go a long way, that makes food sufficient for unexpected guests, and that makes 30 minutes of focused early-morning work produce what hours of afternoon effort cannot.

The Barakah Effect Flow

Barakah is a gift from Allah that enters things, creates a multiplier effect, and brings you closer to Him.

The Author's Journey

Mohammed Faris grew up as a young East African boy with Yemeni roots, often hearing the word "Barakah" from his elders without fully understanding its meaning. Like many Muslims, his basic understanding was that Barakah is "a good thing" and "you should pray for it."

In 2007, while studying at the University of Bristol, he started a blog called "The Productive Muslim" to connect Islamic concepts to productivity science. He stumbled upon a hadith of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him): "O Allah, put Barakah for my nation in the early hours" (Abu Dawud). Three things struck him about this hadith: the Prophet specifically asked Allah for Barakah for his nation; he requested it be attached to a specific time (the early hours); and the narrator, Sakhr Bin Wada'ah, became wealthy by acting on this advice.

This began years of research into the Quran, Prophetic sayings, and Islamic history to understand how one can attract Barakah. His articles on "18 Sources of Barakah" and "Barakah Culture vs Hustle Culture" became reference material quoted by scholars and taught in Islamic schools worldwide. His workshops at Google, Salesforce, American Airlines, and other Fortune 500 companies confirmed that people from all backgrounds hunger for Barakah in their personal and professional lives.

The Book's Structure

The Barakah Effect is divided into two parts. Part One aims to understand what Barakah is, what it is not, and how to measure it. It also addresses why most of us feel like we have lost Barakah in our volatile, uncertain, and fast-changing world.

Part Two delves into the Barakah Culture Manifesto, with each chapter exploring an element of the manifesto and how it brings Barakah to our lives. Throughout the book, traditional Islamic knowledge is combined with modern research, practical tips, and stories to make Barakah actionable and tangible.

By reading this book, you will be able to: (1) Understand the concept of Barakah and its vast application in life and business. (2) Apply the Barakah Culture Manifesto and framework to your personal and professional life. (3) Develop practical routines and processes to embed Barakah Culture in your daily life.

Seven Intentions Behind the Book

The author shares seven sincere intentions (niyyah) for writing this book. In Islam, actions are judged by their intentions, so establishing clear intentions before any significant undertaking is a way of seeking Barakah from the very beginning.

His intentions include: writing a book about Barakah that pleases God and calls people to a spiritual way of living; fighting the Hustle Culture approach to productivity; reconnecting the Muslim world to its heritage of Barakah Culture; making the concept easy to understand with practical examples; encouraging professionals and organisations to adopt Barakah Culture; popularising the concept to Muslims and non-Muslims alike; and making the book a form of sadaqah jariyah (ongoing charity) after his death.

These seven intentions model the very first principle of the Barakah Culture Manifesto: start every action with sincere intention. When we approach our work, our studies, and our relationships with sincere intentions dedicated to Allah, we open the door for Barakah to enter.

The Barakah Culture Manifesto

The Barakah Culture Manifesto is the heart of this book. It is a declaration of principles that, when lived, attract divine Barakah into every aspect of life. The manifesto states:

Bismillah. Start every action with sincere intention, and ask Allah for help. Life is not about you, it's about serving your Lord with excellence. Thank Allah for what you have, and seek His sustenance beautifully. Be like a gardener: focused, hardworking, gentle, and trust Allah with the results. Cherish your parents, and treat them with mercy and compassion. Plant seeds whose harvest will benefit generations to come. Pray and persist for your goals, until you achieve them or are guided to what's better. Remember death often to stay focused on what matters. Send abundant salawat upon Prophet Muhammad. Alhamdulillah. The Barakah Culture Manifesto

Each line of this manifesto corresponds to a chapter in Part Two of the book. Together, they form a complete system for living a life infused with Barakah -- one where you achieve more with less, not through hustle, but through alignment with the Divine.
