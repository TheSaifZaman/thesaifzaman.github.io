Be Like a Gardener — Focused, Hardworking, Gentle and Trust Allah with the Results

~22 min read Manifesto Element

1

The Gardener Metaphor

The author uses a beautiful metaphor to capture the Barakah approach to work and life: be like a gardener. A gardener plants seeds, waters them diligently, removes weeds, ensures proper sunlight, and then -- crucially -- waits. They cannot force a seed to sprout. They cannot pull the stem upward to make it grow faster. They do their part with excellence and patience, and then they trust the growth process.

Similarly, in your career, relationships, and spiritual life, you plant seeds through effort and intention, you nurture them through consistency and care, you remove the weeds of distraction and sin, and then you trust Allah with the harvest. This is profoundly different from Hustle Culture, which demands that you control every outcome, that you "manifest" results through sheer force of will.

The Gardener's Lifecycle (Step Through)
Step 1 / 5
Plant seeds: set your intentions, make du'a, and begin your effort with bismillah.
Gardener Mindset vs Controller Mindset

The gardener finds peace in effort, while the controller finds stress in outcomes they cannot guarantee.

From Seed to Harvest: What You Control vs What You Don't

Focus your energy on the four circles within your control. The outcome's timing, form, and scale belong to Allah.

2

Tawakkul: Trust in Allah

Allah says in the Quran: "And whoever relies upon Allah -- then He is sufficient for him. Indeed, Allah will accomplish His purpose. Allah has already set for everything a decreed extent" (Quran 65:3). Tawakkul is one of the most powerful spiritual states a Muslim can cultivate, and it is the beating heart of the gardener metaphor.

However, there is a critical distinction between Tawakkul and tawakul (laziness). Tawakkul means doing your absolute best -- studying for the exam, preparing the business plan, training for the competition -- and then leaving the results to Allah. Tawakul means sitting at home, doing nothing, and saying "Allah will provide." Umar ibn Al-Khattab rebuked people who neglected work under the guise of trust in Allah, telling them: "The sky does not rain gold and silver."

The Prophet (peace be upon him) illustrated this perfectly when a man asked whether he should tie his camel or trust in Allah. The answer? "Tie your camel and then trust in Allah." Both elements are essential: maximum effort followed by complete reliance.

The Tawakkul Spectrum

The balanced centre is true Tawakkul: neither laziness nor anxious control, but diligent effort coupled with serene trust.

The Tied Camel: Two Halves of One Whole

Neither half alone is sufficient. The Sunnah model is to tie the camel (take action) AND trust Allah (surrender the outcome).

Key distinction: Tawakkul = full effort + trust in Allah for the outcome. Tawakul (laziness) = no effort + false reliance. The gardener plants and waters (effort), then trusts Allah to make the seed grow (tawakkul). They do not leave the field empty and expect a harvest.
3

Focused and Hardworking

Islam deeply values effort and work. The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) worked as a shepherd in his youth and as a merchant before prophethood. Abu Bakr As-Siddiq, the first Khalifah and the Prophet's closest companion, continued his trading even after assuming leadership of the Muslim community, until the companions convinced him to accept a stipend so he could dedicate himself to governance.

The key insight from the gardener metaphor is that hard work must be directed -- focused on what truly matters rather than scattered across a thousand tasks. A gardener does not plant seeds everywhere randomly; they choose the right soil, the right season, and the right crops. Similarly, a Muslim professional should be strategic about where they invest their energy.

This is the antithesis of the busyness that Hustle Culture glorifies. Being busy is not the same as being productive. Running in circles is exhausting but covers no ground. The gardener is both hardworking and purposeful -- every action has a reason, every effort is directed toward growth.

Focused Effort vs Scattered Busyness

A gardener does not scatter seeds randomly. They choose the right soil, the right season, and the right crop.

The Strategic Gardener's Decision Matrix

Like a gardener choosing where to plant, invest your best energy where impact and purpose align. Weed out what drains you without return.

4

Gentle Growth vs Toxic Growth

The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "The most beloved deeds to Allah are the most consistent ones, even if they are small" (Bukhari and Muslim). This hadith is a direct rebuke of the sprint-and-crash cycle that defines modern productivity culture. Barakah Culture favours steady, sustainable growth over burnout-inducing sprints.

A tree that grows slowly develops deep roots. It can withstand storms, droughts, and harsh winters because its foundation is solid. A tree that shoots up rapidly often has shallow roots -- it looks impressive but collapses at the first serious test. The same principle applies to careers, businesses, Islamic knowledge, and personal development.

Deep Roots vs Shallow Roots (Step Through)
Year 1 / 4
Year 1: Both trees begin growing. The rapid tree is already taller, but the steady tree is building deep roots underground.

Gentle growth also means being kind to yourself. Islam does not demand that you destroy your body and mind in pursuit of goals. The Prophet (peace be upon him) told Abdullah ibn Amr, who was fasting every day and praying all night: "Your body has a right over you, your eyes have a right over you, your spouse has a right over you." Balance and gentleness are inherent to the Islamic approach.

Rights Over You: The Balance Wheel

The Prophet (PBUH) taught that every part of your life has a right over you. True productivity honours all of them.

Sprint-Crash Cycle vs Steady Progress

Over time, the steady path (Istiqamah) always outpaces the sprint-crash cycle. Consistency compounds; burnout deducts.

5

Patience (Sabr) in the Process

Sometimes results take longer than expected. Sometimes they come in forms you did not anticipate. Sometimes the seeds you planted years ago sprout when you have almost forgotten about them. The gardener understands this deeply -- they do not dig up seeds to check on their progress. They trust the process and remain patient.

Sabr (patience) in Islam is not passive endurance. It is an active, conscious choice to continue doing what is right even when results are invisible. The Quran mentions Sabr over 90 times, and Allah promises: "Indeed, the patient will be given their reward without account" (Quran 39:10) -- meaning the reward for patience is immeasurable.

In practical terms, this means: do not quit a beneficial project because the results are slow. Do not abandon a good habit because you do not see immediate transformation. Do not lose faith in your du'as because they are not answered on your timeline. The gardener who planted an olive tree knows it may take years before it bears fruit -- but when it does, it will feed generations.

The Olive Tree Timeline: Patience Pays Off

An olive tree takes 3-5 years to produce its first fruit, 15-20 years to reach full production, and can live for over 1,000 years.

The Three Types of Sabr (Step Through)
Type 1 / 3
Sabr in Obedience: the patience to consistently fulfil what Allah commands, even when it is difficult -- like praying Fajr on time or fasting long summer days.
Don't Dig Up Your Seeds

Do not dig up your seeds to check progress. Trust the process, keep watering, and let growth happen in Allah's time.

The olive tree test: An olive tree takes 3-5 years to produce its first fruit and 15-20 years to reach full production. Yet it can live for over 1,000 years. Barakah Culture asks: are you planting olive trees or chasing seasonal flowers? The slow, patient investment yields the most enduring harvest.

Chapter 9 Summary

  • Be like a gardener: Plant seeds (intention), nurture them (effort), remove weeds (distractions), and trust Allah with the harvest (outcome).
  • Tawakkul vs tawakul: True trust in Allah means maximum effort plus reliance on Him -- not laziness disguised as spirituality.
  • Focused hard work: Islam values directed effort over scattered busyness. Plant in the right soil, not everywhere at once.
  • Gentle growth over sprints: "The most beloved deeds to Allah are the most consistent, even if small." Steady progress builds deep roots.
  • Sprint-crash is toxic: Burnout-inducing sprints may look impressive but collapse at the first real test.
  • Be kind to yourself: Your body, eyes, and family have rights over you. Balance is inherent to the Islamic approach.
  • Sabr is active, not passive: Patience means continuing to do right even when results are invisible -- Allah rewards it without limit.
  • Plant olive trees: Slow, patient investment yields the most enduring harvest. Do not dig up seeds to check progress.

Key Terms

Tawakkul
Trust in Allah -- doing your best and leaving the outcome to Him.
Tawakul
False reliance/laziness -- neglecting effort while claiming to trust Allah.
Sabr
Patience -- active, conscious perseverance in doing what is right, especially when results are slow.
Istiqamah
Steadfastness/consistency -- maintaining course on the straight path without deviation.
Barakah Culture
The Islamic alternative to Hustle Culture -- steady, intentional, God-centred growth.
