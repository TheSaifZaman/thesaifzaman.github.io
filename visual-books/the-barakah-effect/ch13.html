Chapter 13

Remember Death to Stay Focused on What Matters

5 Lessons 10-Question Quiz ~22 min read Manifesto Element
13.1

Death as a Lens, Not a Fear

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) said: "Remember often the destroyer of pleasures" (Tirmidhi). This "destroyer of pleasures" is death — and the Prophet's instruction was not to dread it but to use it as a clarifying lens that sharpens your focus on what truly matters.

In Islam, remembering death is not morbid or depressing. It is a tool of profound clarity. When you hold the reality of death before your eyes, something remarkable happens: the trivialities of dunya (worldly life) fall away. The petty arguments, the obsession with status, the endless chase for more — all of it shrinks to its true size when measured against eternity.

Death consciousness works like a filter. It separates the essential from the trivial, the eternal from the temporary, and the meaningful from the meaningless. When you remember that your time on this earth is limited and unknown, you become fiercely intentional about how you spend every hour.

The modern world does everything possible to make us forget death — anti-aging industries, euphemistic language ("passed away" instead of "died"), hiding death from public view. Islam takes the opposite approach: it brings death into focus, not to paralyze but to prioritize.

Death as a Clarifying Filter
When you remember death, the trivial falls away and only what has eternal value remains.
13.2

The Akhirah Perspective

This life — the dunya — is described in the Quran as "play and amusement" (Quran 47:36). This doesn't mean life has no value, but it means that compared to the Akhirah (Hereafter), this world is like a drop in the ocean. When you zoom out to the eternal timeline, a lifetime of 60, 80, or even 100 years is almost nothing.

The Akhirah perspective does not diminish the importance of this life — it reframes it. This life is a test, a field for planting seeds whose harvest will be reaped in the next world. Every moment of hardship, every missed opportunity, every apparent setback takes on a different meaning when seen through the lens of eternity.

The Prophet said: "If the world were worth even the wing of a mosquito to Allah, He would not have given a disbeliever a drink of water from it" (Tirmidhi). This powerful comparison puts worldly success and failure in their proper place. The person who "has it all" in this life but has nothing in the Hereafter is truly poor. And the person who struggles in this life but builds wealth in the Akhirah is truly rich.

This perspective is liberating, not depressing. It frees you from the anxiety of "keeping up," the fear of missing out (FOMO), and the despair of temporary setbacks. When you know that eternal success is what matters, you can face worldly challenges with a peace that confuses those around you.

Perspective Shift: A lifetime of struggle followed by eternal Paradise is an infinitely better deal than a lifetime of luxury followed by eternal loss. The Akhirah perspective transforms how you evaluate every decision, every sacrifice, and every apparent failure.
13.3

Five Before Five

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) gave one of the most powerful time-management counsels in history: "Take advantage of five before five: your youth before your old age, your health before your sickness, your wealth before your poverty, your free time before your busyness, and your life before your death." (al-Hakim)

Each of these five pairs represents a window of opportunity that, once closed, may never reopen. Youth gives way to old age, health deteriorates, wealth can vanish overnight, free time disappears as responsibilities mount, and life itself has an expiration date that is known only to Allah.

This hadith is the ultimate anti-procrastination formula. It asks: "What are you waiting for?" The youth who delays worship until retirement. The healthy person who postpones Hajj until "later." The wealthy person who keeps saying "I'll give charity next year." The person with free time who wastes it on entertainment. The person who assumes they have decades left.

Each "before" is a reminder that the window will close. The question is not whether these transitions will happen, but whether you will have used the current window wisely when it does.

Five Before Five: Windows of Opportunity
"Take advantage of five before five" — the ultimate anti-procrastination counsel from the Prophet.
13.4

Death as Motivation, Not Depression

A common misconception — both among Muslims and non-Muslims — is that thinking about death leads to passivity, depression, or withdrawal from life. The historical record proves the exact opposite. The Companions of the Prophet (Sahabah) were perhaps the most productive generation in human history, and they lived with death constantly on their minds.

The Companions built a civilization, conquered territories larger than the Roman Empire, established systems of governance, advanced science and learning, and transformed the social fabric of the Arabian peninsula — all while maintaining the most intense worship schedules imaginable. They didn't retreat from life; they engaged with it more fiercely because they knew their time was limited.

Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) is reported to have said: "Take account of yourselves before you are taken to account." This is the motivational dimension of death consciousness: if you know you will be questioned about how you spent your time, health, wealth, and knowledge, you become extraordinarily intentional about how you use them.

The difference between healthy death consciousness and morbid fixation is simple: healthy death consciousness makes you more engaged with life, not less. It makes you prioritize better, love more deeply, forgive more readily, and work more purposefully. Morbid fixation, on the other hand, paralyzes — and this is not what Islam teaches.

Key Insight: The most productive people in Islamic history were also the most death-conscious. Death awareness did not make them retreat — it made them strive harder for what truly matters. Remember death to live fully, not to stop living.
13.5

Practical Remembrance

Remembering death is not merely a theoretical exercise — it requires practical habits that keep the reality of mortality fresh in your consciousness. The Prophet and his companions had concrete practices that we can adopt.

Visit graves. The Prophet said: "I had prohibited you from visiting graves, but visit them, for they remind you of the Hereafter" (Muslim). Standing before graves is a visceral reminder that every person once had plans, ambitions, and worries — and now they are beneath the earth, carrying nothing but their deeds.

Write a will (wasiyyah). The Prophet said: "It is not permissible for a Muslim who has something to bequeath to sleep two nights without having their will written" (Bukhari & Muslim). Writing a will forces you to confront the reality that you might not wake up tomorrow — and to plan for that possibility.

Ask yourself daily: "If I died today, what would I regret not doing?" This simple question, asked sincerely every morning, has the power to restructure your entire day. If the answer is "I would regret not calling my parents," pick up the phone. If it's "I would regret not praying Fajr," set an alarm. Let death consciousness drive action, not anxiety.

Plan your legacy. What will people say about you after you've gone? What beneficial projects will continue after you? What knowledge have you shared? What children have you raised? Legacy planning is not morbid — it's the most forward-thinking activity you can engage in.

Death-Awareness Exercises: (1) Visit a graveyard monthly. (2) Write and update your will. (3) Ask yourself daily: "If I died today, what would I regret?" (4) Send du'a for the deceased. (5) Plan your Sadaqah Jariyah. (6) Reconcile with anyone you have a grudge against — today, not tomorrow.

Chapter 13 — Key Takeaways

  • "Remember often the destroyer of pleasures" — death consciousness is a clarifying lens that separates the essential from the trivial.
  • The Akhirah perspective reframes every hardship and setback: this life is a planting field, and the harvest is eternal.
  • "Five Before Five" is the ultimate anti-procrastination formula: youth, health, wealth, free time, and life itself are all windows that will close.
  • The Companions were the most death-conscious AND the most productive generation — death awareness drives engagement, not retreat.
  • Practical death remembrance includes visiting graves, writing a will, daily reflection on regrets, and legacy planning.
  • Healthy death consciousness makes you more engaged with life — more intentional, more loving, more purposeful.

Key Terms

Hadim al-Ladhdhaat
"The Destroyer of Pleasures" — the Prophet's term for death, used to encourage frequent remembrance.
Akhirah
The Hereafter; the eternal life after death. Islam teaches that this life is temporary and the Akhirah is the true, permanent abode.
Muhasabah
Self-accounting; taking stock of one's deeds and intentions. Umar said: "Take account of yourselves before you are taken to account."
Wasiyyah
An Islamic will; the Prophet said no Muslim should sleep two nights without having their will written if they have something to bequeath.
