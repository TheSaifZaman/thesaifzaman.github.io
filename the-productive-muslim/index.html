The Productive Muslim

by Mohammed Faris Where Faith Meets Productivity

Summary

Productivity is defined as Focus × Energy × Time (towards a beneficial goal). It's about making smart, continuous choices to maximise your potential and achieve beneficial results. The chapter dispels four myths:

  • Productivity is NOT about being busy — a productive person should look less stressed.
  • Productivity is NOT an event — it's a daily process and lifestyle.
  • Productivity is NOT boring — it's about knowing when to have fun and when to work hard.
  • You can't ALWAYS be productive — even machines break down at constant high speed.

Action Items

Summary

Islam uniquely brings three dimensions to productivity that modern science lacks:

  1. Purpose-Led Productivity: Two divine roles — being a slave of Allah (abd) and a vicegerent (khalifah) on earth. Productivity becomes purposeful, not pointless.
  2. Value-Driven Productivity: Islamic values like amanah (trust), sidq (honesty), ihsan (excellence), adl (justice), rahma (mercy) provide an internal moral compass.
  3. Soul-Guided Productivity: Islam nourishes the soul through daily prayers, fasting, and remembrance, maintaining the body-mind-soul balance.

The chapter also addresses 5 misconceptions killing Muslim productivity: neglecting dunya, sole reliance on dua, passive sabr, limiting righteousness to worship, and fatalistic view of destiny.

The updated Islamic definition: Productivity = Focus × Energy × Time (towards maximising reward in the Hereafter)

Summary

Spiritual productivity is the link between spirituality and productivity, achieved through barakah — the attachment of divine goodness to a thing. Three dimensions explored:

I. Spiritual Energy

Sources of spiritual energy that fuel productivity:

Taqwa (God-Consciousness)

Being constantly conscious of Allah's presence. Strengthens character and enables wise decisions. "If the people had believed and been mindful, We would have showered them with blessings." [7:96]

Tawakkul (Trust in Allah)

"Tie your camel AND have tawakkul." Take all means, then trust Allah with results. The bird goes out empty, returns full.

Shukr (Gratitude)

"If you are thankful, I will give you more." [14:7] Gratitude boosts positive energy and overcomes greed.

Sabr (Patience)

Active, not passive — like a cactus surviving the desert AND bearing fruit. Provides spiritual strength during trials.

Ihsan (Excellence)

"Worship Allah as though you see Him." Striving for perfection in all work as if presenting it to Allah.

Acts of worship for energy: Salah (5 daily "certainty anchors" + wudhu refresh), Dhikr (remembrance gave Ibn Taymiyyah extraordinary strength), Sadaqah (charity boosts happiness and well-being), Following the Sunnah, and Istighfar (seeking forgiveness opens doors).

II. Spiritual Focus

Islamic concepts that sharpen focus:

Niyyah (Intention)

Clarify WHY before every action. "Take care of your intentions and actions, leave the outcome to Allah." God judges intentions + actions, not outcomes.

Halal Lifestyle

Earning, eating, and living halal protects spiritual focus. Haram destroys barakah and makes limbs disobey.

Acts of worship for focus: Focused Salah (the Qibla unifies direction, wudhu sets intention, mindful movements = daily mindfulness), Dua (turns worldly quests into spiritual ones), Quran (memorisation physically improves brain connections), and Istikhara (removes decision stress).

III. Spiritual Time

"Time is Life, not Money." Allah swore by time in Surah Al-Asr. Key concepts:

Value of Time

"Two blessings which many people lose: health and free time." Time is a non-returnable, non-exchangeable gift.

Invest Time to Serve God

Time spent serving Allah yields barakah — the student who served community graduated with same grades BUT richer experience.

Dunya vs. Akhira Time

"Be in this world as if you were a stranger or a traveller." 60-90 years is a drop in eternity's ocean.

Acts of worship for time: Salah structures the day into productive intervals. Tahajjud (night prayer) is the "honour of the believer" — investing spiritual time yields extraordinary barakah.

Summary

Managing the physical body through three pillars: energy, focus, and time.

I. Physical Energy

Managed through Sleep, Nutrition, and Fitness:

  • Sleep: Islam views sleep as a blessing and reminder of death. Approach it as worship with intention, wudhu before bed, night prayer, and prophetic duas. Physically: exercise during day, no eating 3 hours before bed, no screens before sleep. Naps (qaylulah) are Sunnah.
  • Nutrition: Prophetic guidance: "Fill 1/3 food, 1/3 water, 1/3 air." Eat with right hand, say Bismillah. Eat slow-burning energy foods. Intermittent fasting (Mon/Thu) has proven benefits.
  • Fitness: The Prophet encouraged swimming, archery, and horse riding. Regular exercise improves sleep by 45-60 minutes and boosts energy.

II. Physical Focus

Focus = a trainable muscle. Two key strategies:

  • Disconnect: Set "disconnect times" (no devices in morning/evening), find a "solitude zone," unplug notifications, change environment.
  • Simplify: De-clutter your mind (brain dump), organise with a trusted system, simplify your workspace, say NO to over-commitments, delegate.

6-Step Refocus Process: (1) Revert to spiritual routine (2) Brain dump (3) Store in trusted system (4) Choose 3 MITs (5) Schedule 90-min focus sessions (6) Apply Disconnect + Simplify equation.

III. Physical Time

"You CANNOT manage time — you can only manage yourself within time."

  • Measure: Track your time for 3-7 days. Use a Productivity Heatmap to visualise energy levels.
  • Plan: Schedule salah first, then appointments, then naps/meals/exercise, then goal tasks. Front-load hard tasks in the morning.
  • Early Morning Routine: 4AM tahajjud → Fajr → Quran → Plan day → Creative work → Exercise → Breakfast. Barakah is in early hours.
  • Beat Procrastination: Become a PRO (just show up daily), use Pomodoro (25-min bursts), gamify boring tasks, watch for "productive procrastination" trap.

Summary

Going beyond yourself to serve others. Islam is community-based — shahadah, salah, zakah, fasting, hajj are all communal. "You are the best community singled out for people" [3:110].

I. Social Energy

Working with others provides stimulating energy. Even introverts need social energy doses. Islam provides regular social energy through: mosque visits (5x daily), Friday prayers, Eid gatherings, Hajj, community iftars, and neighbourhood ties.

Build positive social structures: (1) Inner circle of close righteous friends (2) Broader network for professional growth (3) Community of shared purpose.

II. Social Focus

Focus on areas where your passion and skills intersect. Daniel Goleman's "Other Focus" and "Outer Focus" apply here — empathy for individuals, awareness of community needs. Work in areas you are passionate about for sustainable social impact.

III. Social Time

Use the Minimum Performance Level (MPL) framework: maintain a baseline in all roles (parent, spouse, employee, community member) so no role collapses. Prioritise time between roles wisely. Balance personal ambition with communal responsibility.

IV. Sustaining Social Efforts

15 tips for sustainable impact: Keep focus on Allah, have a vision, think BIG start small, build structure, sincerity + hard work, seek advice, pray istikhara/duha, don't compromise quality, be professional, thank people, network regularly, do one-on-ones, weekly + daily reviews.

Summary

40% of daily actions are habits, not conscious decisions. Three techniques to change habits:

  1. 30-Day Challenge: Force a new habit loop for 30 days. First 21 days require conscious effort; week 4 becomes easier.
  2. Cracking the Habit Loop: Every habit = Trigger → Routine → Reward. Identify the loop, keep the trigger, change the routine, upgrade the reward.
  3. Replacement Theory: Replace old habits with new ones that give similar satisfaction.

Islam and Habits: Islamic rituals have built-in habit loops — Salah (trigger: adhan, routine: detailed prayer, reward: sins forgiven), Fasting (trigger: day/month, routine: abstain, reward: spiritual/physical gains), Emotional responses (anger: seek refuge → wudhu → change position).

7 Daily Spiritual Habits

1 Pray the Sunnah prayers before/after obligatory salah.
2 Recite remembrances (adhkar) after each salah (5-7 minutes).
3 Morning and evening remembrances (adhkar as-sabah wal-masa').
4 Night prayer (tahajjud / witr) — even 2 units before sleep.
5 Duha prayer (mid-morning, equivalent to charity for every bone).
6 Supplications before sleep (10 minutes for blessed rest).
7 30 minutes of Quran daily with understanding.

The Barakah Effect

by Mohammed Faris More With Less

Summary

Mohammed Faris introduces the concept of barakah as the missing ingredient in modern productivity. After years of running ProductiveMuslim.com, he realised that productivity without barakah leads to burnout and emptiness. The book offers an alternative path: achieving more with less by tapping into divine goodness through faith-based practices.

Action Items

Summary

Barakah is the attachment of divine goodness to a thing — when it occurs in something little, it increases it; when in something much, it benefits it. It's the invisible force that makes some people accomplish extraordinary things with limited resources while others achieve little despite having everything. Barakah manifests in time (getting more done in less time), wealth (money going further), relationships (deeper bonds), and health (sustained energy).

Action Items

Summary

Modern society's obsession with hustle culture, constant busyness, and materialistic metrics of success have disconnected us from the sources of barakah. We replaced quality with quantity, depth with speed, contentment with comparison, and spiritual nourishment with shallow dopamine hits. Sins, neglecting acts of worship, ingratitude, and severing family ties are among the spiritual causes of losing barakah.

Action Items

Summary

Barakah culture is a faith-based alternative to hustle culture. While hustle culture says "more is always better," barakah culture says "more WITH less." It values intention over output, quality over quantity, service over self-promotion, contentment over comparison, and trust in Allah over anxiety. Barakah culture embraces rest, worship, and community as productive — not as signs of laziness.

Action Items

Summary

The Barakah Framework consists of 10 principles (Chapters 5-14), each derived from Quranic verses and Prophetic traditions. Together they form a comprehensive system for living a life filled with divine blessings. The framework is both spiritual and practical — each principle comes with actionable steps that can be integrated into daily life.

Action Items

Summary

"In the Name of Allah" — the simplest yet most powerful phrase. Beginning every action with Bismillah invokes divine assistance and places barakah into the action from its inception. It's the key that opens the door to everything blessed. The Prophet (s) instructed us to say Bismillah before eating, entering the home, starting work, and virtually every activity. Without Bismillah, the shaitan shares in our food and actions.

Action Items

Summary

Intention (niyyah) transforms ordinary actions into worship. Eating becomes worship when you intend to fuel your body for serving Allah. Working becomes worship when you intend to earn halal for your family. Coupled with asking Allah's help (isti'anah), every action gains divine support. "Actions are judged by intentions" is the foundational hadith. Sincerity purifies the action; asking Allah's help attracts barakah into it.

Summary

A paradigm shift from self-centred productivity to God-centred service. Ihsan (excellence) means doing everything as beautifully as possible, as if Allah is watching (because He is). When your focus shifts from "What do I get?" to "How can I serve my Lord excellently?", barakah flows naturally. This chapter connects to the concept of being Allah's khalifah — a trustee whose purpose is service, not self-aggrandisement.

Action Items

Summary

Gratitude (shukr) is a multiplier of blessings: "If you are thankful, I will give you more" [14:7]. Seeking sustenance "beautifully" means through halal means, with dignity, without desperation or compromise. This chapter teaches contentment (qana'ah) — being satisfied with what Allah has given while still striving for more. The combination of gratitude + beautiful striving creates a powerful cycle of barakah.

Summary

The gardener metaphor: a gardener prepares the soil, plants seeds, waters consistently, and trusts Allah for the harvest. They don't pull the plant to make it grow faster. Similarly, we should focus on the process (planting and watering) rather than obsessing over outcomes. This is the essence of tawakkul combined with hard work. Gentleness (rifq) in our approach — with ourselves, our projects, and our timelines — prevents burnout and invites barakah.

Action Items

Summary

Birr al-walidayn (honouring parents) is one of the greatest sources of barakah. Allah placed it right after worship of Him in multiple Quranic verses. Parents are a gateway to barakah in your sustenance, your lifespan, and your own children's treatment of you. Serving parents with mercy is not a burden but a privilege and an investment in eternal reward. Even after their death, you can serve them through dua, charity, and fulfilling their promises.

Summary

Think generationally, not just individually. The Prophet (s) said: "If the Day of Judgement comes and you have a sapling in your hand, plant it." This is about leaving a legacy — sadaqah jariyah (ongoing charity), beneficial institutions, knowledge, and righteous children who continue your work. The early Muslims built civilisations because they planted seeds for generations they'd never meet. This long-term thinking is the opposite of instant-gratification culture.

Dua + persistence is the winning formula. Make dua for your goals persistently, and take consistent action towards them. But remain open to divine redirection — sometimes Allah doesn't give you what you want because He's guiding you to something better. The key is to never stop making dua and never stop working, while holding outcomes loosely. This eliminates anxiety about results and keeps you focused on effort and sincerity.

"Remember the destroyer of pleasures" (death) as the Prophet advised. Remembering death is not morbid — it's the ultimate focus tool. It eliminates trivial distractions, forces prioritisation of what truly matters, and creates urgency for good deeds. When you remember death, petty conflicts dissolve, procrastination fades, and you live with intentionality. Death awareness is the antidote to the "I'll do it later" mentality that kills productivity and spiritual growth.
