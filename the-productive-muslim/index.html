The Productive Muslim

by Mohammed Faris Where Faith Meets Productivity

Summary

Productivity is defined as Focus × Energy × Time (towards a beneficial goal). It's about making smart, continuous choices to maximise your potential and achieve beneficial results. Mohammed Faris emphasises that all three elements must work together — if any one is zero, the entire equation collapses. You could have all the time in the world but without energy and focus, nothing gets done.

The chapter dispels four myths:

  • Myth 1: Productivity = Being Busy. A truly productive person should actually look less stressed, not more. Being busy often means you're doing many things poorly rather than a few things excellently.
  • Myth 2: Productivity is an Event. It's not a one-time seminar high or a New Year's resolution — it's a daily process and a lifestyle of consistent, small improvements.
  • Myth 3: Productivity is Boring. True productivity means knowing when to have fun and when to work hard. It creates freedom, not restriction.
  • Myth 4: You Can Always Be Productive. Even machines break down at constant high speed. Human beings need rest, recovery, and downtime to sustain long-term output.
Real-Life Example

Faris describes a common scene: two coworkers sit in the same office. One is constantly frantic — drowning in emails, attending every meeting, staying late — yet somehow always behind. The other leaves on time, exercises regularly, and consistently delivers high-quality work. The difference? The second person manages focus (deep work blocks, no social media during tasks), energy (sleeps well, eats properly), and time (plans the day around 3 Most Important Tasks). The busy person mistakes activity for achievement.

"Productivity is not about doing MORE things — it's about doing the RIGHT things, in the RIGHT way, at the RIGHT time." — Mohammed Faris, The Productive Muslim
Key Insight from the Book

When you feel unproductive, Faris suggests diagnosing which part of the equation is broken: Am I lethargic? (energy problem) Am I distracted? (focus problem) Am I rushing? (time problem). Each diagnosis leads to a different solution. A student who can't study isn't necessarily lazy — they might be sleep-deprived (energy), sitting next to their phone (focus), or cramming the night before (time). Fix the root cause, not the symptom.

Real-Life Example

Think of a mother who wakes up at Fajr, prays with focus, prepares breakfast mindfully, homeschools her children with patience, and still has energy for her own goals in the evening. Compare her with someone who sleeps through Fajr, scrolls social media for 2 hours, rushes through tasks in panic, and collapses exhausted at night having "done a lot" but achieved little of substance. The first person embodies the Focus × Energy × Time formula; the second is trapped in the "busyness myth."

Summary

Islam uniquely brings three dimensions to productivity that modern science lacks:

  1. Purpose-Led Productivity: Two divine roles — being a slave of Allah (abd) and a vicegerent (khalifah) on earth. Productivity becomes purposeful, not pointless. While secular productivity asks "How do I get more done?", Islamic productivity asks "Am I fulfilling my purpose on earth?"
  2. Value-Driven Productivity: Islamic values like amanah (trust), sidq (honesty), ihsan (excellence), adl (justice), rahma (mercy) provide an internal moral compass. You can be highly productive in haram — a scammer can be very efficient — but values ensure your productivity is righteous.
  3. Soul-Guided Productivity: Islam nourishes the soul through daily prayers, fasting, and remembrance, maintaining the body-mind-soul balance. Western productivity focuses on body and mind alone, but the soul is the engine that drives everything.
"I only created jinn and mankind to worship Me." [Quran 51:56] — This single verse gives every Muslim a crystal-clear purpose that billions of people spend their entire lives searching for. — Referenced in The Productive Muslim, Chapter 2
Real-Life Example: Purpose-Led Productivity

Consider two software engineers. Both are talented, both work hard. Engineer A codes for the salary and the prestige — when the job gets tough, motivation crashes. 5 Misconceptions Killing Muslim Productivity

  1. "Focus on Akhira, ignore Dunya" — Some Muslims believe worldly pursuits are inherently bad. But Umar ibn Al-Khattab said: "Do for your worldly life as if you will live forever, and do for your Hereafter as if you will die tomorrow." Islam commands us to build the earth, not abandon it. The early Muslims were scholars, scientists, traders, and leaders because of their faith, not in spite of it.
  2. "Just make dua, Allah will handle it" — A man asked the Prophet (s): "Should I tie my camel or trust in Allah?" The Prophet replied: "Tie your camel, then trust in Allah." Dua without effort is wishful thinking. A student who prays for good grades but doesn't study is misunderstanding tawakkul.
  3. "Be patient (sabr) and do nothing" — Faris compares true sabr to a cactus — it doesn't just survive the desert passively; it thrives, stores water, and bears fruit in the harshest conditions. Sabr is active perseverance, not passive resignation. Musa (AS) didn't just sit and wait at the Red Sea — he struck it with his staff.
  4. "Righteousness = only worship" — Some limit being a "good Muslim" to prayer and Quran alone. But the Prophet (s) said even smiling at your brother is charity. Feeding your family, doing your job with excellence, removing harm from the road — all are acts of worship when done with the right intention.
  5. "Everything is predestined, why bother?" — This fatalistic view paralyses action. Yes, Allah has written everything, but He also commanded us to strive. The pen has dried, but you don't know what's written — so act as if everything depends on your effort, and trust as if everything depends on Allah.

Summary

Spiritual productivity is the link between spirituality and productivity, achieved through barakah — the attachment of divine goodness to a thing. Faris argues this is Islam's unique contribution to productivity science: the concept that Allah can place blessing in your time, money, and effort so you achieve more with less. Three dimensions explored:

I. Spiritual Energy

Sources of spiritual energy that fuel productivity:

Taqwa (God-Consciousness)

Being constantly conscious of Allah's presence. Strengthens character and enables wise decisions. "If the people had believed and been mindful, We would have showered them with blessings." [7:96]. Taqwa acts as an internal alarm system — it stops you before a bad decision and pushes you towards good.

Tawakkul (Trust in Allah)

"Tie your camel AND have tawakkul." Take all means, then trust Allah with results. The Prophet (s) described the bird that leaves its nest hungry in the morning and returns full by evening — it trusts Allah but still flies out to seek.

Shukr (Gratitude)

"If you are thankful, I will give you more." [14:7] Gratitude boosts positive energy and overcomes greed. Research shows grateful people sleep better, have stronger immunity, and experience more positive emotions.

Sabr (Patience)

Active, not passive — like a cactus surviving the desert AND bearing fruit. Sabr is the spiritual muscle that keeps you going when motivation fades. The Quran mentions sabr over 90 times — it's the most repeated command after belief itself.

Ihsan (Excellence)

"Worship Allah as though you see Him." Striving for perfection in all work as if presenting it to Allah. When a carpenter builds with ihsan, every joint is perfect even where no one will see, because Allah sees.

Real-Life Example: Taqwa in Action

A Muslim accountant discovers a tax loophole that could save her company thousands but involves misrepresenting figures. Without taqwa, the decision is purely financial. With taqwa, she hears the internal alarm: "Allah is watching." She reports honestly. Months later, when the tax authority audits similar companies and imposes massive fines, her company is safe. Taqwa didn't just protect her akhira — it protected her dunya too. As Faris writes, taqwa is not a limitation on productivity; it's a superpower that opens doors you didn't know existed.

Acts of worship for energy: Salah (5 daily "certainty anchors" + wudhu refresh), Dhikr (remembrance gave Ibn Taymiyyah extraordinary strength), Sadaqah (charity boosts happiness and well-being), Following the Sunnah, and Istighfar (seeking forgiveness opens doors).

Real-Life Example: Dhikr & Extraordinary Strength

Faris tells the famous story of Ibn Taymiyyah, who was once asked about his incredible productivity — he wrote over 500 volumes of books, taught, gave fatwa, and even fought in battles. His secret? He said: "My dhikr (remembrance of Allah) is my breakfast. If I don't have my breakfast, my strength leaves me." He would sit after Fajr prayer remembering Allah until sunrise. Students reported he seemed to have supernatural energy and focus, all fuelled by this spiritual practice. Modern neuroscience confirms that meditation and mindfulness practices (which dhikr essentially is) reduce cortisol, improve focus, and increase mental resilience.

II. Spiritual Focus

Islamic concepts that sharpen focus:

Niyyah (Intention)

Clarify WHY before every action. "Actions are judged by intentions." God judges intentions + actions, not outcomes. A single action can earn zero reward or immense reward depending entirely on your niyyah.

Halal Lifestyle

Earning, eating, and living halal protects spiritual focus. Haram destroys barakah and makes limbs disobey. The Prophet (s) described a man making dua desperately, but his food, drink, and clothing were all haram — "How can his dua be accepted?"

Real-Life Example: The Power of Niyyah

Two people go to the gym at the same time. Person A goes to look good on social media. Person B sets the intention: "I'm strengthening my body to have more energy for worship, to serve my family better, and to fulfil my duty as Allah's khalifah." Same action, same sweat — but Person B earns reward with every rep. Faris emphasises that niyyah is like a "spiritual GPS" — it tells you why you're doing what you're doing, and without it, you're driving without a destination. A student who studies with the intention to use knowledge for serving the ummah experiences a different quality of focus than one who studies purely for grades.

Acts of worship for focus: Focused Salah (the Qibla unifies direction, wudhu sets intention, mindful movements = daily mindfulness), Dua (turns worldly quests into spiritual ones), Quran (memorisation physically improves brain connections), and Istikhara (removes decision stress).

Key Insight: Salah as Daily Mindfulness

Faris makes a brilliant observation: Salah is the original mindfulness practice. Wudhu is a physical "reset" that transitions you from distraction to focus. Standing in prayer, you face one direction (Qibla), recite specific words, perform specific movements — all requiring presence. The modern mindfulness industry charges thousands for what Muslims have free access to five times a day. A focused 5-minute prayer can be more powerful than an unfocused 20-minute one. The Prophet (s) said the one who prays but gets nothing from it except exercise — unfocused prayer — barely counts.

III. Spiritual Time

"Time is Life, not Money." Allah swore by time in Surah Al-Asr — one of the shortest yet most powerful surahs. Faris argues that the common phrase "time is money" devalues time. Money can be earned back; time cannot. Key concepts:

Value of Time

"Two blessings which many people lose: health and free time." Time is a non-returnable, non-exchangeable gift. Every second you waste is a second you'll wish you had back on the Day of Judgement.

Invest Time to Serve God

Time spent serving Allah yields barakah — counterintuitively, giving time to worship doesn't reduce your productive hours, it multiplies them through barakah.

Dunya vs. Akhira Time

"Be in this world as if you were a stranger or a traveller." 60-90 years is a drop in eternity's ocean. This perspective eliminates attachment to trivial matters.

Real-Life Example: The Student Who Served

Faris shares the story of a university student who volunteered 10 hours per week at a local Islamic centre despite a demanding course load. His classmates thought he was crazy — "You don't have time for that!" But at graduation, he had the same grades as his peers, PLUS leadership experience, a strong network, and a deep sense of fulfilment. Meanwhile, classmates who "didn't have time" for community work had spent those same 10 hours weekly on Netflix and social media. The student experienced barakah in his time — Allah blessed the remaining hours because he invested some for His sake.

"By time. Indeed, mankind is in loss. Except for those who have believed, done righteous deeds, advised each other to truth, and advised each other to patience." [Quran 103:1-3] — Surah Al-Asr, the four-ingredient formula to not lose in the currency of time

Acts of worship for time: Salah structures the day into productive intervals — think of Fajr, Dhuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha as "productivity sprints" between anchors. Tahajjud (night prayer) is the "honour of the believer" — investing spiritual time in the last third of the night yields extraordinary barakah. The Prophet (s) and his companions accomplished more before Fajr than most people accomplish all day.

Summary

Managing the physical body through three pillars: energy, focus, and time. Faris stresses that your body is an amanah (trust) from Allah — neglecting it isn't humility, it's betrayal of that trust. I. Physical Energy

Managed through Sleep, Nutrition, and Fitness:

  • Sleep: Islam views sleep as a blessing and reminder of death. Approach it as worship with intention, wudhu before bed, night prayer, and prophetic duas. Physically: exercise during day, no eating 3 hours before bed, no screens before sleep. Naps (qaylulah) are Sunnah — a 20-minute power nap after Dhuhr can restore alertness by 100%.
  • Nutrition: Prophetic guidance: "Fill 1/3 food, 1/3 water, 1/3 air." The Prophet (s) ate with his right hand, said Bismillah, and never criticised food. Eat slow-burning energy foods (dates, olive oil, honey, whole grains). Intermittent fasting (Mon/Thu) has proven scientific benefits — autophagy, improved insulin sensitivity, and mental clarity.
  • Fitness: The Prophet encouraged swimming, archery, and horse riding. Regular exercise improves sleep by 45-60 minutes and boosts energy. Even a 20-minute walk dramatically improves cognitive function.
Real-Life Example: The 1/3 Rule

A doctor at a ProductiveMuslim seminar shared how he suffered from chronic fatigue despite sleeping 8 hours. After applying the prophetic 1/3 rule (stopping when his stomach was one-third full), he lost 15 kg in 6 months, his energy skyrocketed, and his afternoon slump disappeared. He said: "I was spending 14 centuries' worth of prophetic wisdom on medicine when the cure was in a hadith all along." Modern science confirms that overeating diverts blood flow to digestion, causing brain fog — exactly what the Prophet (s) warned against 1400 years ago.

Real-Life Example: The Prophet's Sleep Schedule

Faris outlines the Prophet's (s) sleep pattern: Sleep shortly after Isha (around 9-10 PM), wake in the last third of the night for tahajjud (around 3-4 AM), pray Fajr, then begin the day. This aligns perfectly with modern sleep science — sleeping in 90-minute cycles, with the deepest restorative sleep occurring in the first half of the night, and the lightest sleep (easiest to wake from) in the early morning hours. The Sunnah nap after Dhuhr then compensates for any sleep debt, creating a "biphasic sleep" pattern that many peak performers and historical figures (Leonardo da Vinci, Winston Churchill) also followed.

II. Physical Focus

Focus = a trainable muscle. Two key strategies:

  • Disconnect: Set "disconnect times" (no devices in morning/evening), find a "solitude zone," unplug notifications, change environment. Faris notes that the Prophet (s) used to retreat to Cave Hira for solitude — disconnection from noise is a prophetic practice.
  • Simplify: De-clutter your mind (brain dump), organise with a trusted system, simplify your workspace, say NO to over-commitments, delegate. A cluttered desk = a cluttered mind.
Real-Life Example: The Disconnect Experiment

Faris describes a young professional who checked his phone 150 times a day (the average!). He challenged himself to a "digital sunset" — no screens after Maghrib. The first week was painful. By week three, he was reading Quran for 45 minutes each evening, having deep conversations with his wife, and sleeping better than he had in years. His morning focus improved so dramatically that he accomplished in 4 hours what previously took 8. He said: "I didn't gain more time; I gained more presence in the time I already had."

6-Step Refocus Process: (1) Revert to spiritual routine (2) Brain dump everything on paper (3) Store in trusted system (GTD, Notion, etc.) (4) Choose 3 MITs (Most Important Tasks) (5) Schedule 90-min deep focus sessions (6) Apply Disconnect + Simplify equation.

III. Physical Time

"You CANNOT manage time — you can only manage yourself within time." This is a paradigm shift. Time flows at the same rate for everyone; the difference is what you do within it.

  • Measure: Track your time for 3-7 days in 20-minute intervals. Use a Productivity Heatmap to visualise energy levels. Most people are shocked to discover they waste 3-4 hours daily on activities they don't even enjoy.
  • Plan: Schedule salah first (non-negotiable anchors), then appointments, then naps/meals/exercise, then goal tasks. Front-load hard tasks in the morning when willpower is highest.
  • Early Morning Routine: 4AM tahajjud → Fajr → Quran → Plan day → Creative work → Exercise → Breakfast. The Prophet (s) said: "O Allah, bless my ummah in their early mornings." Barakah is in the early hours.
  • Beat Procrastination: Become a PRO — just show up daily. Use Pomodoro (25-min bursts). Gamify boring tasks. Watch for the "productive procrastination" trap — reorganising your desk instead of writing that report.
Real-Life Example: The Productivity Heatmap

A university student tracked her energy for one week and discovered her peak hours were 6-10 AM and 4-6 PM, while 1-3 PM was her dead zone. She restructured her schedule: difficult subjects at 6 AM (after Fajr), easy revision at 1 PM, and creative projects at 4 PM. Her grades jumped from B+ to A without studying more hours. She was studying at the right time instead of the conventional time. Faris notes that fighting your biology is pointless — work WITH your energy rhythm, not against it.

Summary

A 5-layer productivity pyramid framework that connects your daily actions to your eternal purpose:

  1. Purpose (Base): Worship Allah — the foundation of everything. Without this, the pyramid crumbles. Every goal must trace back here.
  2. Vision: Your specific contribution to the world. Use the top-down approach (deep reflection on what you want to present to Allah) or the bottom-up approach (explore, try things, and discover your calling through action).
  3. Roles: Father/mother, Muslim, employee, volunteer, citizen, student. Align each role with your vision so no role contradicts another.
  4. Values: Your personal values (e.g., independence, generosity, justice, ihsan) that guide decisions when the path is unclear.
  5. Goals (Top): Set goals across all life areas with 6-month, 1-year, 5-year, 10-year, 20-year timelines. Test every goal against the "Akhira column" — will this help or hurt your eternal life?
Real-Life Example: The Pyramid in Action

Faris illustrates with a Muslim doctor: Purpose: Worship Allah. Vision: "Heal people and reduce suffering in underserved communities." Roles: Muslim, doctor, husband, father, community volunteer. Values: Ihsan (excellence), rahma (mercy), sidq (honesty). Goals: 6 months — complete rural health training; 1 year — volunteer at a free clinic monthly; 5 years — open a charitable clinic in her hometown; 20 years — train 100 doctors who serve the poor. Every daily task connects upward: studying medicine serves the vision, the vision serves the purpose. On hard days, she doesn't ask "Why am I doing this?" — the pyramid answers that question.

Key Insight: The Akhira Column

Faris introduces a powerful filter: for every goal, add an "Akhira column." Want to buy a luxury car? Akhira column: Does this bring me closer to or further from Allah? Want to memorise Quran? Akhira column: Direct investment in eternal life. Want to get a promotion? Akhira column: If it means earning more halal income for my family and giving more charity, yes. If it means compromising values, no. This single filter eliminates 80% of goal-setting confusion. A goal that scores high in dunya but zero in akhira is a bad investment; a goal that scores high in both is golden.

From Goals to Daily Action

Translate goals into daily action using the Taskinator system: Goals → break into MITs (Most Important Tasks) → organise in a Weekly Taskinator (allocate MITs across 7 days) → execute via Daily Taskinator (today's 3 non-negotiable tasks). If you do 3 MITs every day for a year, that's over 1,000 meaningful actions towards your goals. Consistency beats intensity.

Real-Life Example: The Taskinator System

A student wants to memorise 5 juz of Quran in 6 months. She breaks it down: 5 juz = 100 pages = ~17 pages per month = ~4 pages per week = less than 1 page per day. Her Daily Taskinator includes: MIT 1 — Memorise half a page after Fajr. MIT 2 — Review yesterday's portion after Dhuhr. MIT 3 — Recite in night prayer. What seemed overwhelming as a 6-month goal becomes completely manageable as a daily task. This is the power of the pyramid — it takes a massive vision and breaks it into today's action.

Summary

Research shows that 40% of daily actions are habits, not conscious decisions. This means nearly half your day runs on autopilot. The question isn't whether you have habits — it's whether your habits serve you or sabotage you. Three techniques to change habits:

  1. 30-Day Challenge: Force a new habit loop for 30 days. The first 21 days require conscious effort (this is where most people quit); week 4 becomes easier as the neural pathway strengthens. By day 30, the behaviour begins to feel natural.
  2. Cracking the Habit Loop: Every habit follows the pattern: Trigger → Routine → Reward. Identify the loop, keep the trigger, change the routine, upgrade the reward. You can't just delete a habit — you must redirect it.
  3. Replacement Theory: Replace old habits with new ones that give similar satisfaction. Craving social media scrolling? Replace it with reading Quran on your phone. The trigger (boredom) and reward (stimulation) remain; only the routine changes.
Real-Life Example: Cracking the Habit Loop

Faris describes a man who had a habit of watching TV for 3 hours every evening. He mapped his habit loop: Trigger — coming home tired from work and sitting on the couch. Routine — turning on TV. Reward — relaxation and escape. Instead of fighting willpower, he changed the routine while keeping the trigger and reward: he replaced the remote control on the couch with a book. When he sat down tired, the book was there instead. After 3 weeks, he was reading for 2 hours each evening. The reward (relaxation) was the same, but the routine now enriched his mind instead of numbing it.

Key Insight: Islam's Built-in Habit Loops

Faris makes a remarkable observation: Islamic rituals are perfectly designed habit loops. Salah: Trigger = adhan (5x daily, impossible to miss). Routine = detailed physical and verbal prayer sequence. Reward = sins forgiven, inner peace, connection with Allah. Fasting: Trigger = Ramadan / Monday-Thursday. Routine = abstaining from food and desires. Reward = spiritual purification, health benefits, community bonding. Anger management: Trigger = feeling angry. Routine = say "a'udhu billah," make wudhu, sit down or lie down. Reward = calm restored. Islam didn't just give us good habits — it gave us scientifically optimal habit structures 1400 years before behavioural psychology discovered them.

Fasting does NOT kill productivity — it actually enhances it in 5 scientifically-backed ways:

  1. Increases self-awareness: When you consciously resist basic urges (food, water), you become more conscious of all your behaviour and thought patterns.
  2. Facilitates breaking bad habits: Ramadan is a natural 30-day habit challenge. The abstinence from food creates a "reset window" where other habits can be changed more easily.

It's like a garden with 21 types of flowers — the more varieties you cultivate, the more vibrant and blessed the garden becomes.

Summary

A practical blueprint for building a barakah culture in your personal life, family, and organisation. Faris organises it into three layers:

  • Values: Ihsan (excellence), Amanah (trustworthiness), Shukr (gratitude), Tawakkul (reliance on Allah), Rahma (mercy), Adl (justice). These are the non-negotiable foundations.
  • Mindsets: Abundance over scarcity, Service over self, Process over outcome, Contentment over comparison, Faith over fear. These shape how you interpret events and make decisions.
  • Rituals: Morning adhkar together, team/family dua before meals or meetings, weekly family Quran circle, gratitude journaling, Friday family halaqah, beginning projects with Bismillah, ending the day with istighfar. These are the daily and weekly practices that embed barakah culture into the rhythm of life.
Real-Life Example: Barakah Culture in a Family

Faris describes a family that implemented barakah culture rituals: They start every morning with adhkar together at the breakfast table (takes 5 minutes). Before meals, the youngest child says Bismillah for everyone. Every Friday evening, they have a 20-minute family halaqah where one person shares a hadith or Quranic reflection. Before bed, each family member shares one thing they're grateful for. The father reported: "Our home transformed. The children bicker less, my wife and I communicate better, and there's a sense of peace (sakinah) that wasn't there before. We didn't change our house, our income, or our schedule. We changed our culture — and the barakah followed."

Real-Life Example: Barakah Culture at Work

A Muslim-owned tech startup introduced three barakah rituals: (1) Team meeting starts with "Bismillah" and a brief dua. (2) Quarterly "gratitude circles" where team members publicly appreciate each other. (3) A strict no-gossip policy framed as an Islamic value. Within a year, employee retention improved dramatically, workplace satisfaction soared, and productivity increased — all without any expensive management consultants. The CEO said: "Every company talks about 'culture.' We just used the culture Allah already designed for us."
